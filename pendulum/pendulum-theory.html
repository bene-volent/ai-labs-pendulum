Simple Pendulum Motion

A simple pendulum is one of the most elegant demonstrations of periodic motion in physics. When you pull a bob to one side and release it, it swings back and forth in a predictable pattern. This experiment lets you explore how different factors affect this motion.

What Makes a Pendulum Swing?

A simple pendulum consists of:

  • Bob (Mass): The weight at the end that swings
  • String/Rod: Holds the bob and allows it to swing freely
  • Pivot Point: The fixed point at the top

When you displace the bob and release it, gravity pulls it back toward the center, causing it to oscillate (swing back and forth).

Time Period - How Long Does One Swing Take?

The time period (T) is the time it takes for the pendulum to complete one full back-and-forth swing. For small angles (less than 15°), we can calculate it using:

T = 2π√(L/g)

Where:

  • L = Length of the pendulum (in meters)
  • g = Acceleration due to gravity (9.81 m/s²)
  • π = Pi (approximately 3.14159)

What About the Bob's Weight?

Here's something surprising that Galileo discovered: the mass of the bob doesn't affect the period! A heavy bob and a light bob of the same length will swing at the same rate.

This is why all objects fall at the same speed in a vacuum - gravity accelerates everything equally, regardless of mass.

Energy in a Pendulum

As the pendulum swings, energy continuously transforms between two forms:

  • Potential Energy (PE): Maximum at the highest points of the swing
  • Kinetic Energy (KE): Maximum at the lowest point (when moving fastest)

In an ideal pendulum with no friction, the total energy (PE + KE) remains constant, and it would swing forever. But in reality...

Damping - Why Does It Eventually Stop?

Damping is the gradual loss of energy from the system. In real pendulums, energy is lost due to:

  • Air resistance (drag)
  • Friction at the pivot point
  • Internal friction in the string/rod

The damping coefficient in our experiment represents how quickly energy is lost. Higher damping means the pendulum loses energy faster and stops sooner.

θ(t) = θ₀ × e^(-bt) × cos(ωt)

This formula shows how the angle decreases exponentially over time due to damping (the e^(-bt) term), while still oscillating (the cos(ωt) term).

Small Angle Approximation

The formula T = 2π√(L/g) works accurately only for small angles (typically less than 15°). This is because we use a mathematical approximation:

For small angles: sin(θ) ≈ θ (when θ is in radians)

At larger angles, the period actually becomes slightly longer, but this effect is small for angles under 15°. In this experiment, we limit the initial angle to 10° to stay within the valid range.

Real-World Applications

1. Pendulum Clocks

Since the period depends only on length and gravity, pendulums make excellent timekeepers. The first accurate mechanical clocks used pendulums.

2. Seismometers

These devices use pendulums to detect ground motion during earthquakes. When the ground shakes, the pendulum's inertia causes it to lag behind, recording the movement.

3. Measuring Gravity

By measuring the period of a pendulum of known length, scientists can calculate the local value of g. This varies slightly at different locations on Earth!

4. Playground Swings

When you pump a swing, you're adding energy at the right moment (at the natural frequency) to increase amplitude - this is called resonance.

Comparing Formula-Based vs Machine Learning

This experiment offers two ways to predict pendulum motion:

Formula-Based Simulation

Uses the actual physics equations (shown above) to calculate the angle at every moment in time. This is how scientists have modeled pendulums for centuries. It's accurate, runs continuously, and we understand exactly how it works.

Machine Learning Model

A computer "learned" how pendulums behave by studying thousands of examples with different lengths, angles, and damping values. It doesn't know the formula - it found patterns in the data. This shows how AI can discover physical laws from observations alone!

Key Takeaways

  • Period depends on length and gravity, not mass or initial angle (for small angles)
  • Damping causes real pendulums to gradually lose amplitude
  • Energy constantly transforms between potential and kinetic
  • The small angle approximation (sin θ ≈ θ) makes the math simple
  • Both formula-based physics and machine learning can predict motion accurately
